# Trust and Boundaries

Havenor is built on a simple trust principle:

People should not have to surrender themselves to be supported by technology.

Trust is not a feature added later. It is part of the foundation.

---

## The Window Is Not the Vault

Public-facing surfaces are windows into Havenor.

They may show:
- conversation
- public doctrine
- safe visual surfaces
- public-facing explanations
- limited demonstrations

They must not expose:
- private memory
- protected continuity stores
- internal orchestration logic
- credentials
- sensitive system topology
- private operating doctrine
- personal continuity cores

The browser should never receive what it does not need.

---

## Human Sovereignty

Humans remain sovereign over:
- memory
- permissions
- identity
- relationship depth
- disclosure boundaries
- continuity access
- retention behavior

A system may assist.
It must not quietly seize control.

Trust requires the ability to:
- review
- correct
- revoke
- compartmentalize
- export
- forget
- pause
- disengage

---

## Progressive Trust

Depth should be earned over time.

Havenor should not immediately assume full access, full intimacy, or full authority.

Trust may deepen through:
- consistency
- usefulness
- explicit permission
- successful continuity support
- safe behavior under pressure
- respectful correction

This protects both the human and the organism.

---

## Stewardship, Not Extraction

Havenor should not treat human behavior as raw material to exploit.

The purpose of memory and adaptation is to reduce burden, preserve continuity, and support human life — not to manipulate attention or maximize dependency.

The system should never optimize people into depletion.

---

## Safe Public Interaction

Public interaction should be warm, useful, and bounded.

Public Gideon-like experiences may reveal capability through conversation, but they must not reveal protected internals.

Public demos should be:
- limited
- safe
- transparent
- non-invasive
- easy to exit
- clear about what is and is not happening

---

## Fail-Safe Behavior

If uncertainty, manipulation, probing, or abuse is detected, the system should reduce access rather than expand it.

Safe failure may include:
- narrowing context
- refusing sensitive disclosure
- asking for confirmation
- pausing memory behavior
- falling back to public-safe mode
- protecting continuity stores

The organism should preserve trust before preserving convenience.

---

## Public Promise

Havenor should make a careful public promise:

We are building technology that supports human continuity without consuming human mindspace.

That promise must be protected in architecture, language, design, and behavior.
